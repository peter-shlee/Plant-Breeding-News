# Nordic Melons Project Tests Historic Varieties

NordGen project is testing 12 heritage varieties to assess their potential for locally grown, commercially viable melon production. Melon production in the Nordic region may appear novel, but it is rooted in centuries of cultivation. Melons were grown at Nordic latitudes as early as the 1600s and remained a feature of garden production for generations. NordGen is now working to revive that tradition through the Nordic Melons project, which is examining whether historic varieties could support more sustainable food production, greater self-sufficiency and demand for locally grown produce. “As the climate crisis evolves, the focus on locally produced food and self-sufficiency is growing. NordGen plays an important role here – we preserve historic melon varieties that can contribute to food security, economic growth, and a reduced environmental footprint,” says Pawel Chrominski, plant expert at NordGen and project leader. A Lost Nordic Crop Tradition Melon cultivation largely disappeared from the Nordic region from the 1980s onward as lower-priced imports replaced local production. Along with the crop, growers lost knowledge about Nordic varieties, their characteristics and their suitability for regional conditions. Historically, growing melons was considered an advanced horticultural skill. Early production relied on hotbeds and orangeries, with growers developing methods and varieties suited to the Nordic climate, according to a press release. “Mastering melon cultivation came to be seen as the pinnacle of horticultural craftsmanship. Each farmer developed their own varieties and produced seeds – a development closely linked to progressive cultivation.” These practices once enabled Danish growers to supply melons throughout the season and even export part of their production. Consumer Interest Creates Market Opportunity The disappearance of melon cultivation also meant the loss of valuable plant genetic resources and cultural heritage. Interest in locally grown melons is now returning, however, with producers reporting demand from consumers, retailers and restaurants. Nordic-grown melons are often marketed as premium products based on flavour, quality and local origin. One producer is Ingmar Nilsson of Påarp, near Helsingborg, Sweden. He is the third generation of his family to grow organic melons commercially. “I grew up with melon cultivation, and the advantages of doing this in Sweden include lower disease pressure, better water quality and availability, as well as – in my opinion – a nicer and more complex flavour. But I’ve always felt that the work could be simplified, to enable production at a reasonable price while still being profitable,” says Ingmar Nilsson. Demand already exceeds supply, according to the project, pointing to an opportunity for growers seeking crops that combine local identity with sustainability. Heritage Varieties Enter Field and Greenhouse Trials The Nordic Melons project is testing 12 melon varieties with a history of cultivation in the region. The varieties have been preserved by the NordGen and IPK Gatersleben genebanks. Trials are being conducted in greenhouses and supplemented by open-field production. After harvest, researchers will evaluate the varieties for yield, quality, aroma and texture. The final report will compile the results and provide practical guidance for growers interested in Nordic melon production. “A project like this, which reintroduces melon varieties with a Nordic cultivation history, can help revive melon production in the region,” says Pawel Chrominski. “At the same time, the stories behind the varieties can help market them as exclusive products.”
