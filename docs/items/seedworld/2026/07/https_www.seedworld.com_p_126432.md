# The Seed Sector 2045: Climate Adaptation Becomes Breeding’s New Baseline

How climate change is reshaping breeding priorities, resilience traits and crop performance under variable conditions. For most of the past century, plant breeding operated under a relatively stable climatic framework. Breeders improved yield, disease resistance and quality traits within a predictable range of environmental conditions. That stability is now disappearing. Climate volatility is rapidly becoming the new normal, and it is reshaping the priorities of breeding programmes around the world. Across continents, weather extremes are already testing agricultural systems. Rice fields in South Asia are facing periodic drought. Olive groves in southern Europe are under pressure from prolonged heat waves. At the same time, northern European regions increasingly experience heavy rainfall and flooding. For plant breeders, these changes are not distant projections but daily realities that influence selection decisions. Climate adaptation is therefore becoming the new baseline for breeding. Instead of optimizing crops for average conditions, breeders must increasingly design varieties that perform under instability. From Yield Optimisation to Resilience Historically, breeding focused primarily on maximizing yield under favourable conditions. While yield remains essential, resilience is now gaining equal importance. Crops must withstand drought, heat stress, erratic rainfall and shifting pest pressures. Climate change is also influencing disease dynamics. Warmer temperatures are expanding the geographical range of many pests and pathogens, while extreme weather events can weaken plant defences and create favourable conditions for disease outbreaks. As a result, resistance breeding is becoming more complex, often requiring stacked resistance traits and broader genetic diversity. Water management is another critical challenge. Many cropping regions already face declining groundwater levels and increasing irrigation constraints. Breeding for improved water-use efficiency and deeper root systems is becoming a priority across multiple crops. This shift does not mean abandoning yield improvement. Rather, breeders increasingly aim to maintain stable yields under stress conditions. New Tools Accelerate Climate-Focused Breeding Advances in breeding technology are helping accelerate the development of climate-resilient crops. Several approaches are already transforming how breeders tackle environmental stress. Genomic prediction models now allow breeders to identify genetic combinations associated with drought tolerance or heat resilience earlier in the breeding cycle. By analysing large genomic datasets, these models can predict which crosses are most likely to produce resilient offspring. High-throughput phenotyping has also become a powerful tool. Drones equipped with multispectral cameras can monitor thousands of breeding plots, measuring plant temperature, canopy structure and water stress indicators. This allows breeders to detect subtle stress responses that would be difficult to observe in traditional field trials. Gene editing technologies are also opening new possibilities . Precision editing tools can modify specific genes involved in stress tolerance, potentially accelerating the development of crops that withstand extreme environmental conditions. These tools are shortening breeding cycles and expanding the range of traits breeders can target simultaneously. How Prepared are Different Regions? Climate adaptation is not unfolding evenly across regions. North America and parts of South America benefit from large-scale farming systems, faster adoption of technology and fewer regulatory constraints, allowing quicker deployment of climate-resilient varieties. Asia combines rapid innovation with strong public-sector breeding, although exposure to extreme weather remains high. Europe faces a more complex balance. Strong environmental ambitions and regulatory frameworks shape breeding priorities but can also slow the pace of innovation and deployment. In Africa, the challenge is different again. Climate exposure is high, while access to improved seed and breeding infrastructure remains uneven, making resilience a critical priority. The result is a fragmented global landscape, where adaptation capacity varies as much as the risks themselves. Examples Already Emerging Climate adaptation is not only a research objective. It is already shaping breeding programmes across major crops. In maize, breeding programmes in several regions have developed varieties capable of maintaining yield under prolonged drought conditions. These varieties incorporate multiple physiological traits, including improved root architecture and more efficient water use. In wheat, researchers are increasingly targeting heat tolerance during the flowering stage, a period particularly sensitive to temperature spikes that can drastically reduce grain formation. Rice breeding programmes in Asia are also developing varieties capable of surviving temporary flooding, an increasingly common phenomenon in monsoon regions where rainfall patterns have become more unpredictable. These examples illustrate how breeders are responding to specific climate risks in different cropping systems. A Systems Perspective Climate adaptation in breeding cannot be considered in isolation from farming systems. The interaction between genetics, soil management, crop rotations and input use will increasingly determine resilience. For example, varieties designed for low-input systems or regenerative agriculture may rely more heavily on root development, nutrient-use efficiency and beneficial interactions with soil microbiomes. In many regions, climate adaptation will therefore involve aligning breeding strategies with broader shifts in agricultural practices. European agriculture illustrates this complexity particularly well. Climate pressures coincide with stricter environmental regulations , including efforts to reduce pesticide use, improve water quality and restore biodiversity. Future varieties must therefore perform under both climatic stress and tighter input constraints. Preparing for the Next Two Decades Looking ahead, climate adaptation will likely remain the dominant driver of breeding priorities. Climate models suggest that extreme weather events will become more frequent in many agricultural regions over the coming decades. This will require continuous innovation in stress tolerance traits. Seed companies may need to rethink breeding strategies accordingly. This could include expanding multi-location testing networks to capture a wider range of environmental conditions, investing more heavily in genomic and phenotyping technologies, and integrating climate modelling into breeding decisions . Partnerships may also become increasingly important. Collaboration between breeders, climate scientists, agronomists and data specialists can help translate environmental insights into improved varieties. The ultimate goal remains unchanged: delivering genetic gain to farmers. But the definition of success is evolving. In a world of climatic uncertainty, the most valuable varieties may not always be those with the highest yield potential, but those capable of delivering stable performance when conditions become unpredictable. The implications for the seed sector are clear. What was once considered a breeding objective among many is becoming a central strategic priority. Companies that anticipate these shifts early — and invest accordingly — will be better positioned to navigate increasing environmental uncertainty. This will require more than incremental adjustments. It will mean rethinking breeding targets, strengthening data and modelling capabilities, and aligning genetics with evolving farming systems and regulatory expectations. The question is no longer whether climate adaptation will reshape breeding, but how prepared the sector is to respond. What Does This Mean For Seed Companies?
